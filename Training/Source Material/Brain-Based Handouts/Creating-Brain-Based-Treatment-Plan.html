Tips for Creating a Brain-Based Informed Mental Health Treatment Plan

Brain-Based Practice: From Understanding to Action

Involving Caregivers and Supportive Adults in Treatment

While the individual is the primary client, clinicians may need to consider involving caregivers and other supportive adults in treatment. This approach recognizes that the client's mental health challenges may stem from an environment that does not fully accommodate or understand their brain-based differences, leading to unrealistic expectations. Equipping caregivers and key adults can help create a more understanding and adaptive environment, ultimately reducing symptoms. This collaborative approach is an important consideration at all stages of life, including in adulthood.

Fostering Interdependency Over Independency

The goal of treatment is to create an environment of Interdependency versus Independency. It is important for brain-based informed mental health clinicians to reassess the concepts of "dependency" and "enabling" within treatment and systems.

Recognizing Caregiver Brain-Based Differences

Recognize that caregivers also may have their own brain-based differences and challenges. Set realistic, brain-friendly goals for caregivers to ensure they are supported in their role.

Assessing Learning Styles of All Participants

A brain-based informed mental health clinician assesses the learning styles of everyone involved in the treatment plan, including the client, caregivers, and other support providers such as teachers. Understanding whether they are visual, auditory, or experiential learners allows for the delivery of information in a way that is most effective for them. This may include using videos, written materials, hands-on practice, or verbal explanations to ensure meaningful engagement and understanding.

Establishing Felt Safety and Trust

An individual with a brain-based difference could have also experienced traumatic events that affect how they trust and connect with others. To foster an interdependent relationship where they feel comfortable accepting help, it is essential to first establish a sense of felt safety with both the clinician and supportive adults. This foundation is crucial for meaningful engagement and effective growth.

Considering Environmental Influences on Treatment

It's important to recognize that while not a formal treatment goal, the environment significantly influences the therapeutic process. Brain-based informed mental health clinicians should consider factors such as lighting, colors, clutter, and transitions, as these impact the client not only in the therapy office but also in other settings like home, school, and the workplace. A well-designed environment can promote regulation, reduce stress, and support skill development, enhancing the effectiveness of treatment.

Building Strength-Based, Developmentally Appropriate Goals

When developing the treatment plan, it is important to review the assessment, focusing on the individual's brain-based strengths and challenges to ensure the goals are appropriate.

  • Encourage the individual to express their own goals and preferences, ensuring their voice is central to the process.
  • While treatment plans often emphasize areas for improvement, it's equally important to center them around an individual's strengths. Incorporating natural abilities and interests into goals enhances learning, fosters growth, and makes challenges more manageable.
  • Treatment objectives should align with their current cognitive and emotional development, making them both attainable and meaningful.
  • Set goals that are aligned with the client's developmental stage, not chronological age. Each person has a unique developmental timeline, so interventions should be tailored accordingly.
  • Recognize that individuals may struggle to apply skills across different settings. Work with the whole support system to ensure consistency, understanding, and integration of skills across all environments (home, school, therapy settings).
  • Adjusting the pace of treatment is important, as individuals with brain-based challenges might require additional time to develop new skills. Their specific challenges may require simplifying treatment goals and breaking them into manageable steps. Goals should be realistic, outcome-oriented, and open-ended rather than time-restricted, allowing for steady progress and lasting change.
